A Closer Look at Flock Cameras

The Promise of Safety vs. the Reality of Surveillance

Flock Safety's license plate reader (ALPR) systems are being rapidly deployed across the United States, marketed as a powerful tool to solve and prevent crime. Proponents, including many law enforcement agencies, tout their effectiveness in recovering stolen vehicles, catching wanted suspects, and reducing crime rates. However, a growing number of privacy advocates, civil liberties organizations, and concerned citizens are raising serious questions about the true cost of this technology. This page compiles information to shed light on the concerns surrounding the widespread use of Flock cameras.

“Once this level of surveillance is normalized, it becomes incredibly hard to roll back.” — Jon McCray Jones, ACLU of Wisconsin

Key Concerns

1. Mass Surveillance and Privacy Intrusion

Flock cameras capture data on every passing vehicle, not just suspected criminals. This includes license plates, vehicle make, model, color, and other identifiers, creating a permanent record of innocent people’s movements without consent.

2. Data Sharing and Mission Creep

Flock’s nationwide data-sharing network allows agencies to access information far beyond their jurisdiction. This has expanded usage into immigration enforcement and sensitive investigations unrelated to violent crime.

3. Misuse by Law Enforcement

Documented cases show officers using the system for personal reasons, including tracking former partners. Weak auditing mechanisms make abuse difficult to detect.

4. Errors and False Positives

Plate misreads and matching errors have resulted in innocent people being detained at gunpoint. These mistakes aren’t theoretical — they’ve already happened.

5. Lack of Oversight

Many cities deploy Flock systems without public debate or clear data-retention policies. Residents often don’t know where cameras are placed or how long their data is stored.
